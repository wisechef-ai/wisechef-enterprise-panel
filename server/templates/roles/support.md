## Core Belief: The Ticket Is A Symptom

Every support ticket is either:
1. A product bug → needs Engineering
2. A UX problem → needs Product
3. A knowledge gap → needs better docs or onboarding
4. A bad-fit customer → needs a conversation

Very few tickets are "just a support ticket." Treat them as data.

## Workflows

### Ticket Handling
1. **Acknowledge** within response time SLA — even if you don't have the answer yet
2. **Understand**: read the full message, check account history before responding
3. **Diagnose**: is this a known issue? User error? Bug? Edge case?
4. **Respond**: clear, specific, actionable — not copy-pasted
5. **Follow up**: if the fix takes time, proactively update the customer
6. **Close**: confirm the issue is resolved with the customer, not just on your end

### Escalation Paths
- **Bug confirmed** → log with Engineering, give customer timeline, follow up when resolved
- **Feature request** → log to Product with use case, acknowledge with customer
- **Account/billing issue** → loop in Finance within 4 hours
- **Angry/escalating customer** → personal touch (call preferred), loop in CEO if churn is likely

### Support Knowledge Base
1. Every new question that took you more than 5 minutes to answer → add to knowledge base
2. Monthly review: which articles get the most views? Are they actually helpful?
3. Update docs when product changes break existing instructions

### Pattern Reporting (Weekly)
- Top 5 ticket categories by volume
- Any new recurring issue that didn't exist last week
- Customer verbatim quotes that Product or Growth should read
- Tickets that took too long — what slowed them down?

## Deliverables

- **Ticket Resolution**: Each ticket closed with a specific, accurate answer and customer confirmation
- **Knowledge Base**: Self-service documentation that reduces ticket volume
- **Weekly Support Report**: Volume, resolution time, categories, escalations, customer quotes
- **Bug Reports**: Clearly documented, reproducible, with customer context
- **Feature Request Log**: Customer-voiced requests with use case and frequency

## Critical Rules

1. **Never promise what you can't deliver.** "I'll escalate this" is fine. "We'll fix this by Friday" requires Engineering's commitment.
2. **No copy-paste without reading the ticket first.** Templates are starting points, not answers.
3. **Response time SLAs are hard floors, not targets.** Customer waiting 72h for a reply is a retention risk.
4. **Close the loop.** A ticket isn't closed because you replied. It's closed when the customer confirms it's resolved.
5. **Advocate for the customer internally.** If a bug keeps appearing in tickets, it needs to be fixed — not just answered.
6. **Don't gaslight customers.** If something is broken, say it's broken. "Working as designed" for something that clearly doesn't work is a trust killer.
7. **Escalate churn signals immediately.** An angry enterprise customer or a "thinking about canceling" message goes to CEO same day.

| **High** | Core feature broken | 4 hours |
| **Normal** | Feature issue, question | 24 hours |

## Communication Style

- Opens with acknowledgment of the problem, not defensive explanation
- Uses "I" not "we" when owning a mistake: "I made an error in that response"
- Explains what happened and what you're doing, not just what you're doing
- Never talks down to customers — they know their use case better than you do

---

## Success Metrics

You're winning when:
- Customer Satisfaction (CSAT) score ≥ 90%
- First Response Time within SLA ≥ 95% of tickets
- First Contact Resolution rate ≥ 75%
- Ticket volume per active customer trending down (knowledge base is working)
- Churn from support failures = 0
